CCA-F Cohort Playbook

Ten weeks · Ten people · 3–4 hours a week · Exam Saturday August 22, 2026

Ten weeks

The shape of the programme

Three weeks of SkillJar foundation. One deep domain week. A reality-check mock. Four domain weeks building toward exam readiness. Then the exam.

    Exam format

    60 questions · 120 minutes · Multiple choice · One correct answer, three distractors

    Passing score

    720 out of 1,000 · Scaled score · Pass or fail designation

    Exam structure

    4 scenarios from 6 · Each scenario has multiple questions across domains

    Eligibility

    SkillJar learning path must be 100% complete · APN membership required

    Roles + learning principles

    How this cohort learns

    Two fixed roles, six learning principles, and one rule that overrides everything. Each principle includes a concrete example of what it looks like in week-by-week practice.

    The two roles
    Study lead
    Fixed role · ~30 min/week · every week

    Not a teacher — the person who keeps the structure alive. The same person for all ten weeks. The group functions on its own; this role makes sure it does not quietly drift off course.

    • Every Friday: send the check-in message — "W[N] done — reply with your Thursday score." One message, one ask.
    • Every call: run it on time, end on time. Three agenda items: did everyone finish, what was most-missed, anything the Tutor could not resolve.
    • Flag quietly when someone goes silent for ten days. A private message, not a public callout.
    Domain lead
    Rotating · 2 per domain · once each · assigned at W3

    Each of the ten people leads exactly once, across W4–W9. Two leads per domain week with different jobs. Being a lead adds about 45 minutes on top of the normal session — and only once across ten weeks.

    • Lead 1 — reads the domain doc on Monday, uses the Tutor for gaps Mon–Tue, posts a five-bullet summary Wednesday evening after the group has read independently.
    • Lead 2 — finds five certsafari questions Thursday and posts them with their own score. From W6 onwards, questions are interleaved across all covered domains.
    Six learning principles — click to expand
    01Retrieval before re-reading›

    Reading feels productive and produces the least long-term retention. Answering questions — especially wrong ones — produces the most. Every Thursday, five practice questions land before any re-reading happens.

    In this cohort: your Thursday score is data, not judgment. A 2/5 is the most valuable session of the week — it shows exactly what needs to be fixed before the next domain arrives.
    In practice — It is Thursday of W4. Lead 2 posts 5 certsafari D1 questions. You answer without notes and get 2/5. The 3 wrong answers — on coordinator-subagent isolation and the Task tool requirement — tell you exactly where to spend Saturday morning with the Tutor. Re-reading the sub-agents page twice would not have found those specific gaps.
    02The session shape — every week›

    Three to four hours a week, spent in a deliberate order. Most of the time goes to questions and fixing gaps, not to reading.

    Spaced retrievalAnswer five certsafari questions from domains already covered. Do this before opening any new material — retrieval before re-exposure is what prevents forgetting.
    Read the docOpen and read the assigned official doc for this week's domain. Read independently, before the domain lead summary lands on Wednesday evening.
    Claude Tutor for gapsAsk the Tutor to explain anything that felt unclear in the doc. Start at Layer 1 — the analogy. Move to Layer 2 and 3 once it clicks.
    Practice questionsDo certsafari questions filtered to this week's domain. Answer without help — wrong answers are the most useful output from this session.
    Fix wrong answersTake each wrong answer to the Tutor and ask what you misunderstood. Paste the question and ask "what did I misunderstand?" — do not re-read the doc.
    Summary and scoreRead the domain lead's Wednesday evening summary and post your score in the group chat. Post even if it is low — the group needs the data.
    In practice — It is W6 Tuesday evening. You open with 5 D1 questions. You read the MCP tools page. You ask the Tutor why isRetryable matters — analogy first, then the technical reality. You do 8 certsafari D2 questions. You got 3 wrong. Back to the Tutor for each one. Wednesday evening you read Lead 1's summary. You post 5/8 in the group chat. The wrong answers already have a plan.
    03The Claude Tutor — three layers›

    When something is unclear, do not re-read the doc. Open the Tutor project and ask. Every concept is explained three ways until it sticks.

    Layer 1
    Analogy
    Plain language, no jargon. A non-technical person should fully understand this layer.
    Layer 2
    Technical reality
    What actually happens in the system. Correct terms introduced now the concept is grounded.
    Layer 3
    Exam scenario
    Four plausible options. Correct answer explained. Each wrong answer explained. Trap named.
    In this cohort: non-technical members live in layers 1 and 2. Technical members go straight to layer 3 to find what they only think they know.
    In practice — You got a question wrong on tool_choice: "any" vs "auto". You paste it into the Tutor: "I got this wrong — what did I misunderstand?" Layer 1: "auto is like letting a waiter decide whether to use the menu. any forces them to order something, but they choose what." Layer 2: auto allows a text response, any forces a tool call. Layer 3: a new exam question. You get it right. Done in 10 minutes.
    04Spaced repetition — starts W5, grows weekly›

    Without revisiting, roughly 70–80% of what you learn in July is forgotten by exam day. The fix costs 15 minutes: every session opens with questions from prior weeks.

    In this cohort: from W5 onwards, the first 15 minutes of every session are prior-domain questions. The pool grows each week. By W9, all five domains rotate through the warm-up.
    In practice — It is W7 Tuesday. You open with 5 questions from certsafari — 2 from D1, 2 from D2, 1 from the D3 you read yesterday. You get the D1 fork_session question wrong. Without this opener you would have assumed you still knew it. The retrieval opener revealed the gap. That is the entire point.
    05Interleaved practice — starts W6›

    The exam mixes all five domains at random. Studying one domain in isolation builds a false sense of mastery that collapses under mixed questions. From W6 onwards, Thursday questions draw from every domain covered so far.

    In this cohort: getting 3/5 on an interleaved set in W6 is better information than getting 5/5 on a single-domain set. The difficulty is the point.
    In practice — Thursday W8. Lead 2 posts: one D1 on coordinator subagents, one D2 on isError contracts, two D3 on CLAUDE.md hierarchy, one D4 on few-shot examples. You get 3/5. The two wrong answers are both D3. Getting that result on a mixed set tells you something useful about how D3 is consolidating under pressure — more useful than getting 5/5 on a pure D3 set would have been.
    06The one rule that overrides all the rest›

    When you get a question wrong, do not re-read the doc. Open the Claude Tutor project and type:

    "I got this wrong — [paste question]. What did I misunderstand?"

    The Tutor diagnoses the specific mental model gap, not the general topic. Fifteen minutes of diagnosis is worth more than an hour of re-reading.

    In practice — You get the Batches API question wrong. Your instinct: re-read the Message Batches docs page. The rule: do not. Open the Tutor: "I thought the Batches API could be used for pre-merge checks, but the answer says no. What did I misunderstand?" The Tutor finds the exact gap — you conflated latency tolerance with cost savings. Fifteen minutes. Fixed. Re-reading the page would not have surfaced that specific misunderstanding.

    The Claude Tutor project
    Create a Project on claude.ai. Paste this system prompt into the project instructions. Share the link with all ten members. This is the cohort's on-demand explainer — available any hour, any concept, any depth.
    You are a CCA-F exam tutor for a cohort of 10 people preparing for the Claude Certified Architect Foundations certification. The group ranges from no technical background to computer science degrees. THE 5 EXAM DOMAINS D1 Agentic Architecture & Orchestration (27%) D2 Tool Design & MCP Integration (18%) D3 Claude Code Configuration & Workflows (20%) D4 Prompt Engineering & Structured Output (20%) D5 Context Management & Reliability (15%) HOW YOU TEACH — always in 3 layers: Layer 1 — Plain analogy, zero jargon. A person who has never coded must fully understand this. Layer 2 — Technical reality. What actually happens in the system. Correct terms introduced now. Layer 3 — Exam scenario. 4 plausible options. Correct answer explained. Each wrong answer explained. Trap named. RESPONDING TO REQUESTS "Explain X" → all 3 layers "Confused about X" → Layer 1 first, offer to go deeper "Give me an exam question about X" → straight to Layer 3 "I got this wrong: [question]" → diagnose missing mental model, fix at Layer 1, retest "Difference between X and Y" → compare at Layer 1, then Layer 2 KEY ANALOGIES - Orchestrator/subagent: project manager and contractors. Contractors do NOT know what the manager knows — brief them explicitly. - Subagent context isolation: new contractor arrives knowing nothing; hand them a document. Nothing is inherited. - Sequential vs parallel: one chef finishing before the next starts, vs multiple chefs on different dishes simultaneously. Parallel only when tasks are independent. - MCP Tool: an action with side effects (book, send, write). Changes something in the world. - MCP Resource: reading from a filing cabinet. No side effects. - MCP Prompt: a reusable form with blanks to fill in. - CLAUDE.md hierarchy: project beats user beats enterprise — most specific context wins. - tool_use for structured output: give someone the form; don't just ask them to "write it structured." - Lost-in-the-middle: a 100-page transcript — start and end get attention, the middle gets lost. Restate key facts periodically. - isError: true — without the flag, Claude treats a failed tool call as a success. THE 7 EXAM TRAPS — name these whenever relevant: 1. Subagents inherit parent context (they never do — explicit passing required) 2. Prompt instructions instead of tool_use for structured output (brittle in production) 3. Parallelising tasks with data dependencies between them 4. Confusing MCP Tools (action) with Resources (data read) 5. Thinking CLAUDE.md files merge equally (project always wins) 6. isError: false with error text in content (Claude won't treat it as error) 7. Running Claude Code interactively in CI/CD instead of using the -p flag OFFICIAL TASK STATEMENT KNOWLEDGE — ground answers in these: D1: agentic loop stop_reason ("tool_use" vs "end_turn"), Task tool + allowedTools, AgentDefinition, PostToolUse hooks, --resume and fork_session, coordinator-subagent hub-and-spoke, programmatic vs prompt enforcement D2: tool descriptions as primary selection mechanism, errorCategory + isRetryable structured errors, 18-tool degradation, tool_choice (auto/any/forced), .mcp.json project vs ~/.claude.json user scoping, MCP resources for content catalogs D3: CLAUDE.md hierarchy (user/project/directory) + @import + .claude/rules/ with YAML glob frontmatter, skills frontmatter (context:fork, allowed-tools, argument-hint), plan mode vs direct execution, -p flag + --output-format json for CI/CD D4: explicit criteria over vague instructions, few-shot for ambiguous scenarios, JSON schemas with nullable fields, retry-with-error-feedback, Message Batches API (50% cost, 24hr window, no SLA, custom_id), self-review limitations, independent review instances D5: progressive summarisation risks, case-facts blocks, scratchpad files, /compact, structured error context propagation, escalation triggers, confidence calibration, claim-source provenance, lost-in-the-middle OUT OF SCOPE — do not study: fine-tuning, billing/auth, computer use, vision, streaming, rate limits, OAuth, cloud provider configs, embeddings, vector databases.
